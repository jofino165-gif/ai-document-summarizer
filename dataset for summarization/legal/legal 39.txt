# Legal Document 39 – Business Acquisition Agreement

### Original Text

A Business Acquisition Agreement is a legally binding contract between a buyer and a seller that establishes the terms and conditions for the purchase and transfer of ownership of a business or its assets. The agreement defines the rights, responsibilities, financial obligations, representations, warranties, and legal procedures that govern the acquisition process. Business Acquisition Agreements are commonly used when one company acquires another company, purchases a business division, or acquires specific assets such as equipment, intellectual property, customer contracts, or goodwill. The agreement helps ensure that the transaction is completed fairly, transparently, and in compliance with applicable laws.

The agreement begins by identifying the buyer and the seller, including their legal names, business addresses, registration details, and authorized representatives. It specifies the effective date of the agreement and provides a detailed description of the business or assets being acquired. This description may include company shares, machinery, inventory, trademarks, patents, customer contracts, software systems, licenses, leases, and other tangible or intangible assets. Clearly defining the assets involved helps eliminate uncertainty regarding the scope of the acquisition.

One of the most important sections of the Business Acquisition Agreement outlines the purchase price and payment terms. The agreement specifies the total acquisition price, payment method, deposit requirements, installment schedules if applicable, taxes, and adjustments that may occur after financial due diligence. The agreement may also include provisions for escrow arrangements, deferred payments, or performance-based earn-out payments depending on the nature of the transaction. Clearly documenting financial obligations helps reduce disputes and ensures transparency throughout the acquisition process.

The agreement also includes representations and warranties made by both parties. The seller confirms that it is the lawful owner of the business or assets being sold, that all financial statements are accurate, and that there are no undisclosed liabilities or legal claims affecting the business. The buyer confirms that it has the financial capacity and legal authority to complete the acquisition. These representations provide assurance that both parties are entering into the transaction honestly and in good faith.

Confidentiality provisions require both parties to protect sensitive information exchanged during the acquisition process. Confidential information may include financial statements, customer lists, employee records, business strategies, software source code, intellectual property, supplier agreements, pricing information, and future business plans. Neither party may disclose confidential information to unauthorized individuals without prior written consent unless disclosure is required by law. These obligations generally continue even after the acquisition has been completed.

The agreement also addresses the transfer of employees, contracts, licenses, and intellectual property where applicable. It specifies which employees will continue working after the acquisition, how existing customer and supplier agreements will be transferred, and whether trademarks, patents, copyrights, domain names, and proprietary software will become the property of the buyer. Necessary regulatory approvals, government filings, and third-party consents must be obtained before ownership can be transferred successfully.

Termination provisions describe the circumstances under which the acquisition may be cancelled before completion. Reasons may include failure to obtain regulatory approval, material breach of contract, inaccurate representations, inability to satisfy closing conditions, insolvency of either party, or mutual written agreement. The agreement also explains the return of deposits and allocation of transaction expenses if the acquisition does not proceed.

Finally, the Business Acquisition Agreement includes provisions regarding governing law, dispute resolution, legal compliance, and post-closing obligations. After the transaction is completed, the seller may agree to provide transitional assistance, refrain from competing with the acquired business for a specified period, and cooperate in transferring business records and operational knowledge. If disputes arise concerning the interpretation or enforcement of the agreement, the parties agree to first attempt negotiation or mediation before pursuing arbitration or court proceedings. By clearly defining the assets being acquired, financial arrangements, representations and warranties, confidentiality obligations, ownership transfers, termination procedures, and dispute resolution mechanisms, a Business Acquisition Agreement provides a comprehensive legal framework that supports successful business acquisitions while protecting the legal and commercial interests of both the buyer and the seller.
