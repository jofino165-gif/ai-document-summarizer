## Legal Document 8 – Power of Attorney

### Original Text

A Power of Attorney (POA) is a legal document through which one individual, known as the principal, authorizes another individual, known as the agent or attorney-in-fact, to act on their behalf in legal, financial, medical, or personal matters. This document is commonly used when the principal is unable to manage their affairs due to illness, travel, disability, or other personal circumstances. A Power of Attorney allows important decisions to be made without unnecessary delays while ensuring that the principal's interests are protected.

The Power of Attorney begins by identifying the principal and the appointed agent. It includes their full legal names, addresses, and identification details. The document clearly states that the principal is voluntarily granting authority to the agent and confirms that the principal is mentally competent at the time of signing the agreement. Proper identification helps establish the validity of the document and reduces the risk of disputes regarding the identity of the parties.

The document specifies the powers granted to the agent. These powers may include managing bank accounts, paying bills, handling real estate transactions, filing tax returns, signing legal documents, managing investments, or representing the principal before government authorities. In some cases, the authority may also extend to making healthcare decisions if the principal becomes incapable of communicating their wishes. Clearly defining these powers prevents misuse of authority and ensures that the agent acts within the limits established by the principal.

A Power of Attorney may be either general or limited. A General Power of Attorney grants broad authority to manage a wide range of personal and financial matters, while a Limited Power of Attorney restricts the agent's authority to specific tasks or transactions. For example, a principal who is traveling abroad may grant limited authority to an agent to sell a property or manage financial accounts during their absence. Choosing the appropriate type of Power of Attorney depends on the principal's specific needs and circumstances.

The document also explains when the authority becomes effective and when it will end. Some Powers of Attorney become effective immediately after signing, while others become effective only if a specified event occurs, such as the principal becoming mentally incapacitated. The document also outlines situations in which the authority automatically ends, including the death of the principal, revocation of the document, or completion of the assigned responsibilities.

The agent is required to act honestly, responsibly, and in the best interests of the principal. The document emphasizes that the agent has a fiduciary duty, meaning they must avoid conflicts of interest, maintain accurate financial records, and use the granted authority only for lawful purposes. Misuse of authority may result in civil liability, criminal penalties, or removal of the agent by a court.

Many jurisdictions require the Power of Attorney to be signed before witnesses or notarized to ensure its authenticity and legal validity. Proper execution helps financial institutions, healthcare providers, and government agencies recognize the document without unnecessary legal challenges. Some organizations may also require certified copies before accepting the agent's authority.

Overall, a Power of Attorney is an important legal instrument that enables trusted individuals to act on behalf of another person when necessary. By clearly defining the scope of authority, duration, responsibilities, and legal obligations of the agent, the document protects the principal's interests while ensuring that important personal, financial, and legal matters continue to be managed efficiently and lawfully.
