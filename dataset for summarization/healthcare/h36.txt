Patient Name: Karen Mitchell
Age: 43
Gender: Female
Report Date: 27-08-2026

The patient visited the endocrinology department with complaints of excessive fatigue, increased body weight, dry skin, constipation, slow heart rate, hoarseness of voice, hair thinning, and difficulty concentrating for the past five months. She also reported feeling unusually cold even in warm environments. The patient has a family history of autoimmune thyroid disease but no previous diagnosis of diabetes or hypertension.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 118/76 mmHg, heart rate of 58 bpm, temperature of 98.2°F, respiratory rate of 16 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 99%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 12.4 g/dL, white blood cell count of 6,800 cells/µL, platelet count of 278,000 cells/µL. Thyroid function tests showed a Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH) level of **12.6 mIU/L**, Free T4 of **0.5 ng/dL**, and Free T3 of **2.0 pg/mL**. Anti-thyroid peroxidase (Anti-TPO) antibodies were strongly positive.

Lipid profile demonstrated total cholesterol of **232 mg/dL**, LDL cholesterol of **156 mg/dL**, HDL cholesterol of **44 mg/dL**, and triglycerides of **182 mg/dL**. Kidney function tests, liver function tests, fasting blood glucose (94 mg/dL), and HbA1c (5.4%) were all within normal reference ranges. Thyroid ultrasound showed a mildly enlarged thyroid gland with diffuse heterogeneous echotexture, consistent with autoimmune thyroiditis.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Hashimoto's Thyroiditis with Primary Hypothyroidism**. Elevated TSH, reduced thyroid hormone levels, positive Anti-TPO antibodies, and ultrasound findings confirm the diagnosis. Mild hypercholesterolemia is likely secondary to hypothyroidism. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain normal. The patient was advised to begin thyroid hormone replacement therapy, undergo periodic thyroid function monitoring, maintain a balanced diet, engage in regular physical activity, take medication consistently on an empty stomach as directed, and attend routine endocrinology follow-up visits to monitor treatment response and prevent long-term complications.
