Patient Name: Brian Phillips
Age: 59
Gender: Male
Report Date: 25-12-2026

The patient visited the pulmonology department with complaints of progressive shortness of breath, persistent dry cough, chest discomfort, fatigue, and reduced exercise tolerance over the past year. He worked in the shipbuilding industry for more than 30 years with prolonged occupational exposure to asbestos. The patient quit smoking 10 years ago after a 25 pack-year smoking history.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **132/82 mmHg**, heart rate of **86 bpm**, temperature of **98.4°F**, respiratory rate of **20 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **94%** on room air.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **13.0 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **7,300 cells/µL**, platelet count of **288,000 cells/µL**, serum creatinine of **0.9 mg/dL**, blood urea of **18 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **98 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **5.5%**, and liver function tests within normal reference ranges.

Pulmonary function tests demonstrated a **restrictive ventilatory defect** with reduced diffusion capacity. High-resolution CT (HRCT) of the chest revealed **bilateral pleural plaques**, diffuse interstitial fibrosis predominantly affecting the lower lung zones, and areas of subpleural honeycombing, consistent with **Asbestosis**. There was no evidence of pleural effusion or pulmonary malignancy.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Asbestosis**, a chronic occupational interstitial lung disease caused by long-term asbestos exposure. Occupational history, restrictive lung function, and characteristic HRCT findings confirm the diagnosis. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain within normal limits. The patient was advised to avoid further asbestos exposure, participate in pulmonary rehabilitation, receive annual influenza and recommended pneumococcal vaccinations, undergo regular pulmonary function testing and imaging, maintain smoking abstinence, and attend routine pulmonology follow-up visits to monitor disease progression and screen for asbestos-related malignancies.
