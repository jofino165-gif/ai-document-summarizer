Patient Name: Joseph Allen
Age: 64
Gender: Male
Report Date: 25-08-2026

The patient visited the nephrology department with complaints of severe lower back pain radiating toward the groin, painful urination, blood in the urine, nausea, and intermittent vomiting for the past two days. He reported similar episodes three years ago that resolved after treatment for kidney stones. The patient admitted to low daily water intake and a diet high in salt and animal protein.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 148/90 mmHg, heart rate of 96 bpm, temperature of 98.6°F, respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 14.1 g/dL, white blood cell count of 9,800 cells/µL, platelet count of 286,000 cells/µL, serum creatinine of 1.2 mg/dL, blood urea of 22 mg/dL, serum calcium of 10.6 mg/dL, and serum uric acid of 8.2 mg/dL. Fasting blood glucose was 98 mg/dL, HbA1c was 5.4%, and liver function tests were within normal reference ranges.

Urinalysis demonstrated microscopic hematuria, calcium oxalate crystals, and mild proteinuria without evidence of bacterial infection. Non-contrast CT scan of the kidneys, ureters, and bladder (CT KUB) revealed a **7 mm calculus** located in the right proximal ureter with mild hydronephrosis. No additional stones or renal masses were identified.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Right Ureteric Calculus (Kidney Stone)** causing partial urinary obstruction. Imaging findings, hematuria, and characteristic symptoms support the diagnosis. Kidney function remains preserved despite mild hydronephrosis. The patient was advised to increase fluid intake, take prescribed pain medication and alpha-blocker therapy, strain urine to monitor stone passage, reduce dietary sodium and oxalate intake, attend follow-up imaging to confirm stone clearance, and consider urological intervention if spontaneous stone passage does not occur or symptoms worsen.
