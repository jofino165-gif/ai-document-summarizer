Patient Name: Christopher Harris
Age: 66
Gender: Male
Report Date: 10-09-2026

The patient visited the cardiology department with complaints of sudden onset chest pain radiating to the left arm and jaw, severe sweating, nausea, shortness of breath, and dizziness for the past one hour. He has a medical history of hypertension, Type 2 diabetes mellitus, high cholesterol, and a smoking history of 30 years. The patient admitted to poor medication adherence and a sedentary lifestyle.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **168/102 mmHg**, heart rate of **116 bpm**, temperature of **98.7°F**, respiratory rate of **24 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **93%** on room air.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **13.3 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **11,800 cells/µL**, platelet count of **284,000 cells/µL**, fasting blood glucose of **246 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **9.0%**, total cholesterol of **264 mg/dL**, LDL cholesterol of **182 mg/dL**, HDL cholesterol of **37 mg/dL**, and triglycerides of **238 mg/dL**. Kidney and liver function tests were within normal reference ranges.

Electrocardiogram (ECG) demonstrated **ST-segment elevation** in the anterior leads. High-sensitivity cardiac troponin-I level was **markedly elevated at 8.6 ng/mL**. Emergency coronary angiography revealed **100% occlusion of the left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery**. Echocardiography demonstrated a left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) of **42%** with regional wall motion abnormalities.

The physician concluded that the patient has an **Acute ST-Elevation Myocardial Infarction (STEMI)** caused by complete blockage of the left anterior descending coronary artery. ECG findings, markedly elevated cardiac troponin levels, and coronary angiography confirm the diagnosis. Multiple cardiovascular risk factors, including hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, smoking, and poor medication adherence, significantly contributed to the event. The patient was advised to undergo immediate reperfusion therapy, strictly adhere to dual antiplatelet and other prescribed cardiac medications, stop smoking completely, follow a heart-healthy diet, participate in supervised cardiac rehabilitation, maintain strict control of blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol levels, and attend regular cardiology follow-up visits to reduce the risk of recurrent cardiac events.
