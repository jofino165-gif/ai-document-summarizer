Patient Name: Sarah Wilson
Age: 58
Gender: Female
Report Date: 24-09-2026

The patient visited the neurology department with complaints of progressive numbness and weakness in both legs, difficulty walking, loss of balance, blurred vision in the right eye six months ago that resolved spontaneously, and increasing fatigue over the past eight months. She also reported occasional muscle spasms and urinary urgency. There is no history of stroke, diabetes mellitus, or spinal trauma.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **124/78 mmHg**, heart rate of **80 bpm**, temperature of **98.4°F**, respiratory rate of **16 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **99%**.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **13.2 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **6,900 cells/µL**, platelet count of **281,000 cells/µL**, serum creatinine of **0.9 mg/dL**, blood urea of **18 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **94 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **5.4%**, vitamin B12 levels within the normal range, and thyroid function tests within normal reference ranges.

Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) of the brain and cervical spinal cord demonstrated multiple demyelinating plaques involving the periventricular white matter and cervical spinal cord. Cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis revealed positive oligoclonal bands. Neurological examination demonstrated increased muscle tone, hyperreflexia, impaired coordination, and reduced vibration sensation in both lower limbs.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Relapsing-Remitting Multiple Sclerosis (RRMS)**. Clinical symptoms, MRI findings, and positive CSF oligoclonal bands strongly support the diagnosis. Laboratory investigations excluded common metabolic causes of neurological symptoms. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain within normal limits. The patient was advised to begin disease-modifying therapy as prescribed, participate in regular physiotherapy and balance training, maintain regular neurological follow-up visits, manage fatigue through lifestyle modifications, and seek immediate medical attention if new neurological symptoms or relapses occur.
