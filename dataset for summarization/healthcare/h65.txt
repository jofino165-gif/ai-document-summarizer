Patient Name: Olivia Turner

The patient visited the pulmonology department with complaints of persistent dry cough, progressive shortness of breath during physical activity, fatigue, chest tightness, and occasional low-grade fever over the past seven months. She works as a school teacher and has no history of smoking, tuberculosis, or occupational exposure to harmful dust. The patient has a known history of autoimmune thyroid disease.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **122/78 mmHg**, heart rate of **86 bpm**, temperature of **98.7°F**, respiratory rate of **20 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **94%** on room air.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **12.7 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **7,100 cells/µL**, platelet count of **288,000 cells/µL**, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of **48 mm/hr**, C-reactive protein (CRP) of **18 mg/L**, serum creatinine of **0.9 mg/dL**, blood urea of **18 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **96 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **5.4%**, and liver function tests within normal reference ranges.

Pulmonary function testing demonstrated a **restrictive ventilatory defect** with reduced diffusion capacity (DLCO). High-resolution CT (HRCT) of the chest showed **bilateral basal honeycombing, reticular opacities, and traction bronchiectasis**, consistent with pulmonary fibrosis. Autoimmune screening was negative for connective tissue disease–associated interstitial lung disease.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis (IPF)**. Progressive respiratory symptoms, restrictive lung function, and characteristic HRCT findings confirm the diagnosis. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain within normal limits. The patient was advised to begin antifibrotic therapy as prescribed, participate in pulmonary rehabilitation, receive annual influenza and recommended pneumococcal vaccinations, avoid respiratory infections and environmental pollutants, attend regular pulmonology follow-up visits, and undergo periodic lung function testing to monitor disease progression.
