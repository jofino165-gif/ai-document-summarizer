Patient Name: Amanda Lewis

The patient visited the hepatology department with complaints of persistent fatigue, discomfort in the upper right abdomen, mild abdominal swelling, reduced appetite, and occasional nausea for the past three months. She has a medical history of obesity and Type 2 diabetes mellitus for six years. The patient denied alcohol consumption but reported a sedentary lifestyle and frequent intake of high-calorie processed foods.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 142/88 mmHg, heart rate of 84 bpm, temperature of 98.4°F, respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.4 g/dL, white blood cell count of 7,100 cells/µL, platelet count of 274,000 cells/µL. Liver function tests showed ALT (SGPT) of **96 U/L**, AST (SGOT) of **82 U/L**, alkaline phosphatase (ALP) of **138 U/L**, total bilirubin of **1.1 mg/dL**, and albumin of **3.8 g/dL**. Fasting blood glucose was **186 mg/dL**, HbA1c was **8.4%**, and the lipid profile revealed total cholesterol of **236 mg/dL**, LDL cholesterol of **162 mg/dL**, HDL cholesterol of **40 mg/dL**, and triglycerides of **218 mg/dL**.

Kidney function tests demonstrated a creatinine level of **1.0 mg/dL** and blood urea of **19 mg/dL**, both within normal limits. Abdominal ultrasound showed diffuse fatty infiltration of the liver with mild hepatomegaly. Transient elastography (FibroScan) indicated moderate liver fibrosis without evidence of cirrhosis.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD)** with moderate liver fibrosis associated with obesity, poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and dyslipidemia. Elevated liver enzymes and imaging findings support the diagnosis. Kidney function remains normal. The patient was advised to reduce body weight through a calorie-controlled diet, perform regular physical activity, improve blood glucose and cholesterol control, avoid unnecessary medications that may affect the liver, undergo periodic liver function monitoring, and attend regular hepatology follow-up visits to prevent progression to cirrhosis.
