Patient Name: Jennifer Wilson
Age: 34
Gender: Female
Report Date: 28-06-2026

The patient visited the respiratory medicine department with complaints of persistent cough, intermittent wheezing, shortness of breath, and chest tightness for the past four weeks. She reported that her symptoms worsen during the night, after exposure to dust, and while exercising. The patient has a history of seasonal allergies and occasional episodes of breathing difficulty since childhood. She is a non-smoker and has no history of diabetes or hypertension.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 118/74 mmHg, heart rate of 88 bpm, temperature of 98.3°F, respiratory rate of 22 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 95%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.4 g/dL, white blood cell count of 8,300 cells/µL, platelet count of 286,000 cells/µL, and an eosinophil count of 8%, which is above the normal range. Fasting blood glucose was 91 mg/dL and HbA1c was 5.3%, both within normal limits.

Pulmonary Function Test (Spirometry) demonstrated reduced airflow with an FEV1 of 68% of the predicted value, showing significant improvement after administration of a bronchodilator. Chest X-ray revealed no evidence of pneumonia, tuberculosis, or other structural lung abnormalities. Kidney function tests and liver function tests were within normal limits.

The physician concluded that the patient's symptoms, elevated eosinophil count, and spirometry findings are consistent with bronchial asthma. There was no evidence of active lung infection or chronic obstructive pulmonary disease. The patient was advised to use prescribed inhaled medications regularly, avoid exposure to dust, smoke, and other known allergens, maintain regular physical activity as tolerated, monitor symptoms closely, and attend periodic follow-up evaluations to assess disease control and adjust treatment if necessary.
