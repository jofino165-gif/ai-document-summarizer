Patient Name: Maria Lopez
Age: 44
Gender: Female
Report Date: 14-07-2026

The patient visited the cardiology department with complaints of intermittent chest discomfort, shortness of breath during mild physical activity, fatigue, and occasional dizziness for the past two months. She has a medical history of hypertension for six years and obesity. The patient reports a sedentary lifestyle, frequent consumption of high-fat foods, and a family history of coronary artery disease.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 158/98 mmHg, heart rate of 94 bpm, temperature of 98.5°F, respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 97%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.2 g/dL, white blood cell count of 7,100 cells/µL, platelet count of 276,000 cells/µL, fasting blood glucose of 126 mg/dL, HbA1c of 6.4%, total cholesterol of 262 mg/dL, LDL cholesterol of 184 mg/dL, HDL cholesterol of 39 mg/dL, and triglycerides of 238 mg/dL.

Kidney function tests showed a creatinine level of 1.0 mg/dL and blood urea of 20 mg/dL, both within normal limits. Liver function tests were within normal reference ranges. Electrocardiogram (ECG) demonstrated ST-segment depression during exercise stress testing, and echocardiography revealed mild left ventricular hypertrophy with preserved systolic function.

The physician concluded that the patient has early Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) associated with uncontrolled hypertension, dyslipidemia, and prediabetes. Elevated cholesterol levels, abnormal stress test findings, and cardiovascular risk factors significantly increase the likelihood of future cardiac events if left untreated. Kidney and liver function remain normal. The patient was advised to begin aggressive risk-factor management, including blood pressure and cholesterol control, adopt a heart-healthy diet, reduce body weight, perform regular physician-approved exercise, take prescribed medications consistently, and attend routine cardiology follow-up appointments to reduce the risk of heart attack and stroke.
