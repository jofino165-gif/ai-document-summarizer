Patient Name: Daniel Cooper
Age: 53
Gender: Male
Report Date: 09-12-2026

The patient visited the infectious disease department with complaints of persistent fever, productive cough with yellow-green sputum, night sweats, chest pain, fatigue, and increasing shortness of breath over the past three weeks. He has a 20-year history of poorly controlled Type 2 diabetes mellitus and chronic smoking. There was no previous history of tuberculosis.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **126/80 mmHg**, heart rate of **104 bpm**, temperature of **101.4°F**, respiratory rate of **22 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **92%** on room air.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **12.0 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **17,800 cells/µL** with marked neutrophilia, platelet count of **336,000 cells/µL**, C-reactive protein (CRP) of **182 mg/L**, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of **82 mm/hr**, serum creatinine of **1.1 mg/dL**, blood urea of **21 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **228 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **9.1%**, and liver function tests within normal reference ranges.

Chest X-ray demonstrated dense consolidation involving the right middle and lower lobes. Contrast-enhanced CT scan of the chest confirmed extensive multilobar pneumonia without pleural effusion. Sputum culture grew **Pseudomonas aeruginosa**, which was sensitive to appropriate intravenous antibiotics. Blood cultures were negative.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Severe Bacterial Pneumonia caused by Pseudomonas aeruginosa**. Clinical symptoms, elevated inflammatory markers, chest imaging, and microbiological findings confirm the diagnosis. Poorly controlled diabetes and chronic smoking increased the patient's susceptibility to severe respiratory infection. Kidney and liver function remain preserved. The patient was advised to receive intravenous antibiotic therapy, maintain good blood glucose control, stop smoking immediately, perform breathing exercises after recovery, receive annual influenza and recommended pneumococcal vaccinations, and attend regular follow-up visits to ensure complete resolution of the infection.
