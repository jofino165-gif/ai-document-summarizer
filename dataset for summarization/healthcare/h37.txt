Patient Name: Peter Robinson
Age: 58
Gender: Male
Report Date: 29-08-2026

The patient visited the gastroenterology department with complaints of persistent abdominal pain, frequent diarrhea mixed with blood, unintended weight loss of 6 kg over the past four months, fatigue, and an urgent need to pass stools several times a day. He also reported occasional low-grade fever and reduced appetite. The patient has no history of smoking but has a family history of inflammatory bowel disease.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 124/80 mmHg, heart rate of 90 bpm, temperature of 99.2°F, respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **10.9 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **10,800 cells/µL**, platelet count of **348,000 cells/µL**, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of **64 mm/hr**, C-reactive protein (CRP) of **42 mg/L**, serum albumin of **3.2 g/dL**, and fecal calprotectin of **520 µg/g**. Kidney function tests, liver function tests, fasting blood glucose, and HbA1c were within normal reference ranges.

Colonoscopy demonstrated continuous inflammation, ulceration, and friable mucosa extending from the rectum to the descending colon. Multiple biopsies confirmed chronic inflammatory changes consistent with **Ulcerative Colitis** without evidence of colorectal malignancy.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Moderate to Severe Ulcerative Colitis**, an inflammatory bowel disease causing chronic inflammation of the large intestine. Elevated inflammatory markers, anemia, low albumin levels, increased fecal calprotectin, and colonoscopic findings strongly support the diagnosis. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain normal. The patient was advised to begin anti-inflammatory and immunosuppressive therapy as prescribed, maintain adequate hydration and nutrition, avoid foods that worsen symptoms, attend regular gastroenterology follow-up visits, and undergo periodic colonoscopic surveillance to monitor disease activity and reduce the risk of long-term complications.
