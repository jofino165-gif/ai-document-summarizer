Patient Name: Karen Mitchell
Age: 58
Gender: Female
Report Date: 11-12-2026

The patient visited the oncology department with complaints of a painless lump in the upper outer quadrant of the left breast, occasional bloody nipple discharge, breast skin thickening, fatigue, and unintended weight loss of 5 kg over the past three months. She reported that her mother was diagnosed with breast cancer at the age of 61. The patient attained menopause six years ago.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **128/80 mmHg**, heart rate of **82 bpm**, temperature of **98.4°F**, respiratory rate of **18 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **98%**.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **11.8 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **7,400 cells/µL**, platelet count of **306,000 cells/µL**, serum creatinine of **0.9 mg/dL**, blood urea of **18 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **98 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **5.4%**, and liver function tests within normal reference ranges.

Diagnostic mammography and breast ultrasound identified a **3.4 cm irregular spiculated mass** in the left breast with enlarged ipsilateral axillary lymph nodes. Ultrasound-guided core needle biopsy confirmed **Invasive Ductal Carcinoma (IDC)**. Immunohistochemistry demonstrated **Estrogen Receptor (ER)-positive**, **Progesterone Receptor (PR)-positive**, and **HER2-negative** tumor status. CT scan and bone scan showed no evidence of distant metastasis.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Stage II Breast Cancer (Invasive Ductal Carcinoma)** with regional axillary lymph node involvement. Imaging findings, biopsy results, and hormone receptor analysis confirm the diagnosis. Mild anemia is likely associated with chronic disease. Kidney function, liver function, and blood glucose levels remain within normal limits. The patient was advised to consult a multidisciplinary breast oncology team to discuss surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and hormone therapy. Regular follow-up examinations, periodic imaging, and long-term surveillance were recommended to monitor treatment response and detect recurrence.
