Patient Name: Michael Turner
Age: 55
Gender: Male
Report Date: 21-08-2026

The patient visited the cardiology department with complaints of intermittent chest pain during physical activity, shortness of breath, excessive sweating, and fatigue for the past three weeks. The chest discomfort usually lasted for 5 to 10 minutes and was relieved by rest. He has a medical history of hypertension and high cholesterol for eight years and has smoked one pack of cigarettes daily for more than 25 years. His father died of a heart attack at the age of 60.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 162/96 mmHg, heart rate of 98 bpm, temperature of 98.5°F, respiratory rate of 20 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 97%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.7 g/dL, white blood cell count of 7,600 cells/µL, platelet count of 274,000 cells/µL, fasting blood glucose of 132 mg/dL, HbA1c of 6.5%, total cholesterol of 268 mg/dL, LDL cholesterol of 188 mg/dL, HDL cholesterol of 36 mg/dL, and triglycerides of 248 mg/dL. Kidney and liver function tests were within normal reference ranges.

Electrocardiogram (ECG) demonstrated ST-segment depression during exercise stress testing. Coronary angiography revealed approximately 75% narrowing of the left anterior descending (LAD) coronary artery and 60% narrowing of the right coronary artery. Cardiac troponin levels remained within normal limits, indicating no evidence of an acute myocardial infarction.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Stable Coronary Artery Disease (Stable Angina)** caused by significant atherosclerotic narrowing of the coronary arteries. Multiple cardiovascular risk factors, including hypertension, diabetes, dyslipidemia, smoking, and family history, increase the likelihood of future cardiac events. Kidney and liver function remain normal. The patient was advised to stop smoking immediately, follow a heart-healthy low-fat diet, maintain strict control of blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol levels, take prescribed anti-anginal and lipid-lowering medications regularly, participate in supervised cardiac rehabilitation, and attend routine cardiology follow-up visits to reduce the risk of heart attack and disease progression.
