Patient Name: George Bennett
Age: 68
Gender: Male
Report Date: 05-08-2026

The patient visited the neurology department with complaints of sudden weakness on the left side of the body, slurred speech, facial drooping, difficulty walking, and numbness in the left arm and leg that began approximately two hours before arrival at the hospital. He has a medical history of hypertension, Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and hyperlipidemia. The patient admitted to irregular use of prescribed medications and has a smoking history of 25 years.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 186/108 mmHg, heart rate of 98 bpm, temperature of 98.7°F, respiratory rate of 20 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 96%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.2 g/dL, white blood cell count of 8,400 cells/µL, platelet count of 276,000 cells/µL, fasting blood glucose of 248 mg/dL, HbA1c of 9.1%, total cholesterol of 258 mg/dL, LDL cholesterol of 178 mg/dL, HDL cholesterol of 38 mg/dL, and triglycerides of 226 mg/dL. Kidney function tests showed a creatinine level of 1.2 mg/dL and blood urea of 24 mg/dL, while liver function tests were within normal reference ranges.

A non-contrast CT scan of the brain showed no evidence of intracranial hemorrhage. MRI of the brain demonstrated an acute ischemic infarction involving the right middle cerebral artery territory. Carotid Doppler ultrasound revealed approximately 70% stenosis of the right internal carotid artery. Electrocardiogram showed normal sinus rhythm without evidence of atrial fibrillation.

The physician concluded that the patient has an **Acute Ischemic Stroke**, most likely resulting from atherosclerotic disease associated with uncontrolled hypertension, poorly controlled diabetes mellitus, hyperlipidemia, and long-term smoking. Imaging findings confirmed an acute cerebral infarction without hemorrhage. The patient was advised to receive immediate stroke management, maintain strict control of blood pressure, blood glucose, and cholesterol levels, stop smoking completely, participate in supervised stroke rehabilitation, follow a heart-healthy diet, take prescribed medications consistently, and attend regular neurology and cardiology follow-up visits to reduce the risk of recurrent stroke and long-term disability.
