Patient Name: Catherine Hughes

Report Date: 13-11-2026

The patient visited the rheumatology department with complaints of progressive thickening and tightening of the skin over the fingers and hands, cold-induced color changes of the fingers, joint stiffness, heartburn, difficulty swallowing, and increasing fatigue over the past one year. She also reported occasional shortness of breath during physical activity. There was no family history of autoimmune disease.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **138/84 mmHg**, heart rate of **84 bpm**, temperature of **98.5°F**, respiratory rate of **18 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **97%**.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **11.9 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **7,000 cells/µL**, platelet count of **286,000 cells/µL**, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR) of **42 mm/hr**, C-reactive protein (CRP) of **18 mg/L**, serum creatinine of **0.9 mg/dL**, blood urea of **17 mg/dL**, fasting blood glucose of **95 mg/dL**, and HbA1c of **5.4%**. Liver function tests were within normal reference ranges.

Autoimmune testing demonstrated a strongly positive **antinuclear antibody (ANA)** and positive **anti-topoisomerase I (Anti-Scl-70) antibody**. High-resolution CT (HRCT) of the chest showed **early interstitial lung disease** with mild bibasal fibrosis. Esophageal manometry revealed reduced esophageal motility consistent with connective tissue disease involvement. Echocardiography showed normal left ventricular function without evidence of pulmonary hypertension.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Diffuse Systemic Sclerosis (Scleroderma)** with skin, esophageal, and early lung involvement. Clinical findings, autoimmune antibody profile, and imaging studies strongly support the diagnosis. Kidney function remains normal with no evidence of scleroderma renal crisis. The patient was advised to begin immunomodulatory therapy as prescribed, perform regular hand exercises, protect the hands from cold exposure, receive treatment for gastroesophageal symptoms, attend routine rheumatology and pulmonology follow-up visits, and undergo periodic lung function and cardiac evaluations to monitor disease progression.
