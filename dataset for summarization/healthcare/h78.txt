Patient Name: David Anderson

The patient visited the cardiology department with complaints of increasing shortness of breath, swelling of both legs, difficulty breathing while lying flat, waking at night due to breathlessness, fatigue, and reduced exercise tolerance over the past six months. He has a medical history of previous myocardial infarction, long-standing hypertension, Type 2 diabetes mellitus, and hyperlipidemia. The patient admitted to irregular use of prescribed heart medications.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of **158/96 mmHg**, heart rate of **104 bpm**, temperature of **98.6°F**, respiratory rate of **22 breaths per minute**, and oxygen saturation of **93%** on room air.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of **12.2 g/dL**, white blood cell count of **8,100 cells/µL**, platelet count of **292,000 cells/µL**, fasting blood glucose of **224 mg/dL**, HbA1c of **9.2%**, serum creatinine of **1.4 mg/dL**, blood urea of **34 mg/dL**, total cholesterol of **246 mg/dL**, LDL cholesterol of **168 mg/dL**, HDL cholesterol of **38 mg/dL**, triglycerides of **232 mg/dL**, and **B-type Natriuretic Peptide (BNP)** level of **1,280 pg/mL**.

Chest X-ray demonstrated **cardiomegaly** with bilateral pulmonary congestion. Transthoracic echocardiography revealed a **left ventricular ejection fraction (LVEF) of 30%**, global left ventricular hypokinesia, and moderate functional mitral regurgitation. Electrocardiogram showed evidence of an old anterior wall myocardial infarction.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Heart Failure with Reduced Ejection Fraction (HFrEF)** secondary to ischemic heart disease. Elevated BNP, reduced LVEF, pulmonary congestion, and characteristic clinical symptoms confirm the diagnosis. Mild kidney impairment is likely related to reduced cardiac output and chronic disease. The patient was advised to begin guideline-directed heart failure therapy, restrict dietary sodium and fluids as recommended, achieve strict blood pressure and blood glucose control, participate in supervised cardiac rehabilitation, monitor daily body weight, and attend regular cardiology follow-up visits to reduce the risk of hospitalization and disease progression.
