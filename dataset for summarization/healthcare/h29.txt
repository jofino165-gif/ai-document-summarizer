Patient Name: Richard Moore

Report Date: 13-08-2026

The patient visited the urology department with complaints of frequent urination, difficulty initiating urination, weak urine stream, nocturia occurring four to five times per night, incomplete bladder emptying, and occasional lower abdominal discomfort for the past eight months. He denied blood in the urine or significant weight loss. The patient has a medical history of controlled hypertension and no previous history of kidney stones or urinary tract infections.

Vital signs recorded during examination showed a blood pressure of 132/82 mmHg, heart rate of 76 bpm, temperature of 98.4°F, respiratory rate of 16 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 98%.

Laboratory investigations revealed a hemoglobin level of 13.8 g/dL, white blood cell count of 6,900 cells/µL, platelet count of 281,000 cells/µL, serum creatinine of 1.1 mg/dL, blood urea of 20 mg/dL, fasting blood glucose of 98 mg/dL, and HbA1c of 5.4%, all within normal reference ranges. Urinalysis showed no evidence of infection or hematuria.

Serum Prostate-Specific Antigen (PSA) level was 5.8 ng/mL, which is mildly elevated. Digital rectal examination revealed a symmetrically enlarged, smooth prostate gland without palpable nodules. Transrectal ultrasound demonstrated enlargement of the prostate gland measuring approximately 58 mL with no suspicious focal lesions. Post-void residual urine volume was moderately increased.

The physician concluded that the patient has **Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)** causing lower urinary tract symptoms. Clinical findings, prostate enlargement on imaging, and mildly elevated PSA are consistent with benign enlargement rather than prostate cancer. Kidney function remains preserved with no evidence of urinary tract infection or obstruction. The patient was advised to begin prescribed medications to improve urinary flow, reduce evening fluid intake, avoid excessive caffeine and alcohol, undergo periodic PSA monitoring, attend regular urology follow-up visits, and seek medical attention if urinary retention or worsening symptoms develop.
